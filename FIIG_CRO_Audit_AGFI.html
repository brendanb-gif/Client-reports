What's working

Preserve these before testing anything

  • CTA appears early - visitors don't need to scroll to find an action
  • Portfolio returns section (5.95% / 6.49% / 7.05% p.a.) makes outcomes feel tangible and real, with named portfolio types that aid decision-making
  • PwC tax chapter is a credibility signal - currently underused but the asset exists
  • Stat block (1,000+ bonds, 6,000 investors, $4.5bn FUA) provides scale proof. The placement is the issue, not the content
  • Secondary CTA ("Talk with a FIIG expert") gives higher-intent visitors a direct path to conversion without requiring a guide download first

  • Page includes a liquidity explanation ("trade in $10,000 parcels") which directly addresses a common objection for fixed income first-timers
Above the fold

The first 3 seconds are failing cold traffic

📌

Hero bullets sell the product, not the guide

Impact: Medium - creates a message mismatch at the point of arrival
Issue

The 4 hero bullets list the guide's contents: "In depth explanation of Corporate Bonds and fixed Income," "Gain insights from market experts," "Dedicated tax chapter written by PwC," and "$50,000 minimum investment." Three of these are strong content signals. The fourth is a qualification barrier. Grouping them together gives the minimum investment the same visual weight as the guide's value propositions, which dilutes both.

Opportunity

Remove the minimum investment from the hero bullet list and relocate it to a qualification section mid-page. The 3 remaining bullets are doing useful work: they frame the guide as substantive and credible. If additional content bullets are available, "what's inside" specifics (e.g. named chapters, the PwC contribution) reinforce perceived value of the download.

⚠️

$50k minimum disclosed before any value is established

Impact: High - qualifying barrier placed before the persuasion
Finding

The $50,000 minimum investment appears as a hero bullet point alongside the guide content bullets. For cold acquisition, presenting the qualification barrier before building the value case increases early abandonment from visitors who may have been persuadable had they read further. The minimum is important for lead quality. The placement is the issue, not the content.

Opportunity

Move the minimum investment disclosure to a mid-page qualification section, after the value of bonds and FIIG's expertise have been established. Frame it as a service entry point rather than a filter. Additionally, consider adding a qualification field to the form itself - a simple dropdown or checkbox ("I have at least $50,000 available to invest") sets expectations before submission and filters low-quality leads without adding meaningful friction for genuinely interested visitors.

🖼️

Ebook visualisation is present but positioned below the fold

Impact: Medium - directly affects perceived value of the offer at the point of decision
Finding

The page does include a rendered ebook cover image, which is a positive. However, it appears mid-page rather than alongside or above the form. At the point where visitors are being asked to submit their details, the guide's visual representation is not in view. The asset itself is not reinforcing the conversion moment.

Opportunity

Relocate the ebook cover image to sit adjacent to the form in the hero section. Pairing the visual asset with the form at the point of submission increases perceived value and reduces hesitation. If design permits, an open-spread or interior page preview alongside the cover would further reinforce the substantive nature of the guide.

🏅

No credibility signals above the fold

Impact: High - for a financial services offer, trust must be established immediately
Issue

Every trust signal - the $4.5bn FUA stat, the 25-year history, the 6,000 investors figure - sits at the bottom of the page. For paid traffic arriving cold, trust must be established before any scroll happens. This is especially true for a regulated financial services context.

Opportunity

Add a proof bar directly below the hero with 2-3 key stats. Candidates: "$4.5bn funds under advice" / "Over 6,000 Australian investors" / "In operation since 1998." A single PwC logo with "Tax chapter co-authored with PwC" would also carry significant weight at this placement.

Body copy

The page reads like a brochure, not a sales argument

💬

No problem/empathy section

Impact: High - without this, the copy never earns the visitor's trust
Issue

The page moves directly from hero bullets to benefit sections without contextualising the current investment environment or acknowledging the specific position of a high-net-worth investor. For an audience with $50,000 or more available to invest, the decision to look beyond cash and equities is typically driven by a combination of macro conditions (rate movements, equity volatility, inflation) and personal portfolio considerations. The page does not speak to either. Copy that reflects the conditions motivating the search - and demonstrates an understanding of the investor's position - would strengthen relevance and trust before any product claims are made.

Opportunity

Add a brief contextual section above the first benefit block that addresses the current economic environment and what it means for investors seeking income and capital stability. This does not need to be lengthy - 2 to 3 sentences that demonstrate FIIG understands the investor's decision context are sufficient. The goal is to show the page was written for this audience, not a general readership.

📝

Benefits are written as features or negatives

Impact: Medium - copy works, but isn't doing its full conversion job
Finding

Several sections frame bonds by what they're NOT rather than what the investor gains:

Current

"Unlike stocks, a bond's performance isn't linked to a company's share price."

Direction to test

"Your income is locked in from day one. No share price to watch, no earnings season to stress about."

📊

Returns comparison is 5 years out of date

Impact: Medium - weakens one of the page's strongest factual claims
Finding

The claim "Australian bonds have proven to deliver better returns than cash or international shares over the last 20 years" cites Vanguard data to 30 June 2020. That's 5 years old. In a regulated financial context, stale data undermines credibility rather than building it. The claim also has no chart or visualisation to make it land.

Opportunity

Update to current data with a visual comparison chart (bar or line). If a more recent comparable Vanguard or third-party source isn't available, reframe the claim to use FIIG-specific portfolio return data that is dated accurately and refreshed regularly.

🏢

"About FIIG" section leads with the company, not the client

Impact: Low-Medium - missed opportunity to reinforce trust at a late page position
Issue

The credibility section opens: "At FIIG, our clients are at the centre of what we do." This is a textbook example of opening with "we." The visitor at this point in the page is close to converting - this section should reinforce why choosing FIIG is the right decision, not restate company values.

Opportunity

Lead with client outcomes first, FIIG credentials second. For example: "Over 6,000 Australians trust FIIG to manage their fixed income portfolio - here's why." Then move into the 25-year history, expert team, and custody/reporting capabilities.

Social proof

The biggest single gap on the page

⭐

Zero client testimonials

Impact: Critical - this is the most important fix on the page
Issue

There are no testimonials anywhere on the page. For a financial services product with a $50,000 minimum investment, the absence of any client voice is a critical gap. This is a high-consideration, high-trust category. Visitors are asking "have others done this and been satisfied?" and the page provides no answer. The stat block (6,000 investors) implies people are happy - but it doesn't prove it.

Opportunity

Add 2-3 testimonials with real names, photos, and outcome-focused quotes. The best format for this audience: name, state, investment purpose (e.g. "SMSF income"), and a quote that answers "what was it like to invest with FIIG and what did you get out of it." Even a single strong testimonial with a specific portfolio yield outcome would outperform the current zero.

🤝

PwC association is underplayed

Impact: Medium - a brand association this strong shouldn't be a bullet point
Finding

"Dedicated tax chapter written by PwC" appears as one of four hero bullets - same visual weight as "Diversification." In an audience of investors who are making significant wealth decisions, PwC co-authorship is a substantial trust signal. It's being treated like a feature checkbox.

Opportunity

Give it a dedicated callout with the PwC logo visible. Consider positioning it in the proof bar below the hero, or as a standalone credibility section. "The tax chapter you'll actually use - co-authored with PwC" as a section header would carry far more weight than a bullet point.

Paid search message match

Intent is partially served but not capitalised on

🔍

The page sits between two jobs without fully doing either

Impact: High - affects quality score, bounce rate, and conversion rate simultaneously
Finding

This page likely receives traffic from searches like "fixed income investing Australia," "how to invest in bonds," and "bond portfolio Australia." Those searchers are in research mode - they want to understand the asset class. The page partially serves that intent (educational content is present) but is structured as a lead gen page (form, CTA priority). The result is a page that's not quite an education hub and not quite a conversion-optimised lead gen page. It ends up doing neither job as well as it could.

Opportunity

Commit to the lead gen job: the guide is the hook, FIIG's expertise is the reason to trust. Structure the page so that the educational content (returns data, portfolio examples) is in service of the download - not separate from it. Every section should be leading toward "and you can learn all of this in detail in the free guide." The page currently reads like content with a form attached, rather than a form with content supporting it.
